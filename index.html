Navigating the Academic Job Market

MW 2:00 PM PT CSE 4140 UC San Diego

The course offers a thorough exploration of the academic job market, specifically designed for those aspiring to secure assistant professor positions at universities. Whether you are targeting a research-oriented role or a teaching-focused position, this course will provide you with the essential tools, strategies, and insights needed to succeed. You will learn how to craft compelling application materials, including your CV and research/teaching/diversity statement. Throughout this course, you'll receive personalized feedback, engage in mock interviews, and refine your presentation skills.

In addition to mastering the application process, this course gives you a realistic understanding of what it means to be a professor. You will explore topics such as work-life balance, tenure expectations, and navigating departmental dynamics. By the end of the course, you will be well-prepared to present yourself as a strong candidate and confidently embark on your academic career.


Course format: The course will consist of lectures and student activities. Some lectures will be from new faculty members, professors, and department chairs. You will have the opportunity to ask questions and gain valuable insights. Additionally, there will be dedicated sessions where you can work on your own application materials, receiving feedback and guidance from instructors and peers.

Assignment details
Assignment Expectation
Research statement MS students without research experience should analyze an existing statement and identify strengths and weaknesses. Students with research experience should submit a draft research statement, with the expected length scaled to career stage and target institution type.
Teaching statement Students without teaching experience may complete a structured analysis of a faculty teaching statement. Students with teaching experience should submit a 1–2 page teaching statement draft.
Job talk assignment PhD students and MS students with research experience prepare a short job talk and submit slides. Other students complete a written critique of a sample academic job talk, focusing on content, structure, delivery, and recommendations.
Final project Choose a topic such as statement analysis, teaching philosophy development, job-market research, faculty mentoring, or academic writing and publishing. Deliverables include a short proposal, a final report, and a presentation.
Participation and attendance

Active participation is a central part of this course. The goal is not only to understand the academic job market, but also to practice the kinds of critical reading, discussion, and feedback that you will need throughout the application process.

You are expected to arrive prepared, contribute thoughtfully to discussions, and engage seriously with peer review days. Class time is best spent when everyone brings drafts, asks concrete questions, and gives feedback that is candid and constructive.

How to participate well
  • Come prepared: read assigned material before class and think about what was persuasive, confusing, or surprising.
  • Listen actively: pay attention to both content and framing, especially during guest perspectives and peer review.
  • Ask useful questions: good questions move the conversation forward and help others sharpen their thinking.
  • Offer constructive feedback: connect comments to audience, goals, and clarity rather than just preference.
  • Be respectful: treat classmates’ drafts and experiences with professionalism and care.
Absence policy

Students receive one free absence. Missing more than one class should be cleared with the instructor or TA in advance when possible.

Missing a class also means missing the participation credit associated with that meeting.

Best practices

Bring drafts when requested.

Take notes during peer review and guest sessions.

Use feedback sessions to refine material for real applications.
